## Post

ChartMogul published bootstrapped vs. VC-backed SaaS benchmarks this year.

Median annual growth rate for bootstrapped companies: 23%.
For VC-backed companies: 25%.

Two points.

After the pitches, the dilution, the board meetings, and the quarterly pressure — two percentage points.

Here's what almost nobody talks about:

Bootstrapped SaaS companies show higher revenue per employee than equity-backed companies at every ARR level.

Not at some ARR levels. Every. Single. One.

You're growing at essentially the same pace but running leaner, keeping more margin, and not burning capital on headcount that produces less per person than a tighter team would.

The conventional wisdom has always been: you need funding to compete. Without a war chest, you're playing defense while funded competitors outspend you into the ground.

That assumption has a two-point gap and a revenue-per-employee advantage standing against it.

This isn't an argument against raising. There are categories where outpacing the market requires capital, and in those cases raising makes total sense.

But for every founder staring at their cap table wondering if giving up equity will actually get them to their goal faster — there's now real data suggesting staying lean and profitable might produce nearly identical outcomes with fewer strings attached.

The old story: bootstrap until you can't, then raise to survive.

The new story: raise only if the math is undeniably better.

For most SaaS companies, it isn't.
