## Post

The SaaS founders I've watched struggle most aren't bad at acquiring customers.

They're bad at keeping them.

---

Here's a number that quietly reshapes how I think about building:

In top-performing SaaS companies, expansion revenue accounts for 40-50% of new ARR.

Not new logos. Expansion from existing customers who stayed and grew.

---

That means the best SaaS businesses are funding nearly half their growth from people who already said yes once.

The math behind this is brutal in the best possible way:

Acquiring a new customer costs 5-10x more than expanding an existing one. A customer who stays and expands their contract is essentially funding the next acquisition cycle with the margin they generate.

---

NRR (net revenue retention) has become the metric investors prioritize above almost everything else in 2026. The elite companies — Snowflake, Datadog, the names everyone benchmarks against — run 130%+ NRR. That means their revenue grows even if they close zero new deals this month.

The industry median sits at 106%. Enterprise SaaS median is 118%.

A 10-point improvement in NRR adds 20-30% to your company's valuation.

---

Most early-stage founders aren't actively optimizing for NRR yet. They're optimizing for top-of-funnel — new demos, pipeline coverage, MQL volume — because that's where the pressure feels most visible.

That framing makes sense in the first year. But the fastest path to sustainable, fundable growth isn't outspending competitors on acquisition.

It's making the customers you already have so successful that they expand, refer, and never leave.

Pouring more into acquisition while losing ground on retention is filling a bucket with a hole in it.

---

What's your current NRR — and is it a metric you're actively building toward?
