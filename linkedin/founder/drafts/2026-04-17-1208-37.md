## Post

CAC has jumped 40–60% since 2023.

Most founders track it. Fewer track the metric that actually tells you whether you're in trouble.

That metric is CAC payback period.

CAC tells you what you spent to acquire a customer.

Payback tells you when that spend turns cash-flow positive.

Those are very different conversations.

Here's what the 2026 benchmarks look like:

Under 80 days → elite B2B SaaS

Under 12 months → healthy

12–18 months → average

18+ months with no compression plan → concerning

If your payback is 18 months, you're effectively lending money to customers for a year and a half before you've broken even on acquisition.

Every churn event in that window is a clean loss.

This also reframes how you think about retention.

Retention isn't a "customer success team" problem. It's a unit economics problem. Every point of monthly churn extends your payback window and compounds against your LTV:CAC ratio.

And that ratio is moving. The 3:1 LTV:CAC benchmark most founders learned? It's shifting to 4:1 as the new Series A expectation.

Two questions I'd ask any founder right now:

1. What's your CAC payback period? (Not CAC. Not LTV. Payback.)

2. What's your active plan to compress it?

Lower CAC is one lever. Faster expansion revenue is another. Most companies sleep on the second one — and it's often the faster path.
