## Post

TikTok just got hit with a €530 million GDPR fine. Third-largest in EU history.

The charge: employees in China were accessing European user data without proper safeguards. Without telling users. Without a proper risk assessment.

€530 million because compliance was treated as a future problem.

---

Here's what this signals for every company handling user data — not just tech giants:

Regulators are done warming up.

2026 is the year enforcement stopped being theoretical. GDPR fines are at record levels. California just launched a single portal where consumers can wipe their data from every registered data broker in the state. 19 states now have comprehensive privacy laws. The FTC is actively pursuing enforcement actions at a pace that would've been unthinkable five years ago.

The window for operating on a "good enough" compliance posture is closing. Fast.

---

The hard truth: the data practices you built in year one are the ones getting scrutinized in year five.

Most companies I've watched have the same default posture on this. Compliance is a future problem. Legal will figure it out. We'll deal with it when it matters.

That calculus works fine — until your company is the example that teaches everyone else.

---

What I keep coming back to: the companies that built compliance into the foundation from day one are starting to quietly pull ahead of the ones that bolted it on later.

Not just because they're dodging fines.

Because their customers trust them more. Because their data is actually clean and usable. Because they don't have to stop and rebuild their entire data architecture every time the rules change.

Compliance isn't a legal checkbox. It's infrastructure.

The TikTok fine makes headlines. The real story is what happens at every company that skimmed the article and kept scrolling.

The fine covers two violations: Article 46(1) for improper data transfers (€485M) and Article 13 for transparency failures (€45M). TikTok is appealing.
