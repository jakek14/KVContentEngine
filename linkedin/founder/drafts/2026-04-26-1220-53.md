## Post

Most SaaS founders optimize for ARR.

ARR tells you where you are.

NRR tells you if you actually have a business.

---

The median net revenue retention across B2B SaaS right now is 106%.

That means the average company grows its existing customer base by 6% annually through expansion — before acquiring a single new customer.

Top performers hit 120% or higher.

The valuation gap is dramatic. Companies with NRR above 130% trade at 15-20x forward revenue. Those below 100% — where contraction exceeds expansion — trade at 3-5x.

Same revenue figure on the cover slide. Completely different business.

---

Here's what NRR actually measures:

Whether the value your product delivers compounds — or erodes — over time for the customers you already have.

NRR below 100% means you're filling a leaky bucket. Every dollar you spend on acquisition is partially fighting contraction from inside your existing base. You grow, but slower than the math suggests you should.

NRR above 110% means your existing customers are doing meaningful revenue work for you. You could slow acquisition and still grow. That's a compounding business.

---

Most founders don't start thinking hard about NRR until they're preparing for a Series B or a fundraise conversation.

That's too late.

The mechanics that drive expansion — upsell triggers, seat growth, usage-based tier progression, customer success — need to be baked in from the beginning, not retrofitted when someone asks about retention on a pitch deck.

---

ARR is a snapshot. NRR is a trajectory.

If you're only measuring one, you're reading the wrong number.
