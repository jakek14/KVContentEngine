## Post

Cursor 3 just launched. And it quietly changed what a "developer" does.

This isn't an IDE update. It's a rearchitecting of the role.

They rebuilt the interface around one premise: most code will be written by AI agents. Your job is to describe what you want, assign it to an agent, and review the output. Not write the code. Manage the team writing it for you.

---

I've been building with AI-assisted tools since the early Copilot days. What Cursor 3 represents isn't incremental.

Before, the skill was: write clean code, debug well, read your error messages.

Now, the skill is: spec clearly, understand your architecture well enough to know when the agent got it wrong, and make good decisions about what to ship.

That last one is the one nobody talks about enough.

AI agents produce code that looks confident. It compiles. It runs. It passes basic tests.

But "it runs" and "it's correct" are very different things — and the gap between them is where builders who moved too fast get burned.

---

The people winning with Cursor 3 and Claude Code aren't the fastest prompters. They're the people with enough product knowledge to recognize a wrong output before it ships.

60% of all new code written globally in 2026 is AI-generated. Cursor crossed $2B ARR. These aren't niche signals.

The builder who treats AI agents like a team they're managing — writing clear specs, reviewing critically, catching mistakes before they reach production — has a compounding advantage.

The builder who just prompts and deploys is one bad output away from a very public incident.

---

The tools are moving fast. The judgment to use them well is the durable edge.

And judgment still comes from understanding your product deeply enough to know when something's wrong — even when the code looks fine.

That part doesn't get automated.
