## Post

19 U.S. states now have comprehensive data privacy laws.

GDPR fines have crossed €5.88 billion in total enforcement.

New CCPA rules went into effect January 1 — risk assessments, cookie and pixel rules, new requirements for data brokers.

And most early-stage founders still treat data compliance as a "later" problem.

I get it. In the early stages, everything feels like a distraction from growth. Compliance sounds like a big-company concern.

But here's what I've noticed watching companies in this space:

The founders who built compliance-first aren't scrambling right now.

They built something the others didn't — a data practice that doesn't need to be rebuilt every time a new regulation drops.

The first instinct for most startups is to collect everything and figure out the rules later. Move fast. Stay scrappy. Compliance is for the Fortune 500.

Then a new state law passes. An enforcement action makes headlines. An enterprise prospect asks about your data practices in a procurement call. And "figure it out later" becomes an urgent, expensive problem.

The pattern repeats every 18 months.

The founders I respect most reframed it early. Instead of asking "what are we legally required to do," they asked "what data do we actually need, and what's the most defensible way to collect it?"

That question leads somewhere different.

It leads to collecting less. Verifying what you collect. Building consent workflows that don't feel adversarial. Giving users control over their own information.

And when you do that — when you can genuinely say your data practices are clean — it shows up in ways you don't expect.

It shows up in enterprise sales cycles that close faster because procurement doesn't flag you.

It shows up in customer trust that's hard to manufacture but easy to lose.

It shows up in churn rates that are lower, because customers who trust you stay.

The 2026 regulatory wave isn't slowing down. Nineteen states this year, more coming. The EU is tightening cross-border enforcement timelines to 12-15 months. The CCPA added cybersecurity audit requirements phased in over the next four years.

The founders who treat this as a moat — rather than a compliance tax — will be in a very different position three years from now.

Clean data practices aren't just legal hygiene. They're trust.

And trust is one of the few things in SaaS that actually compounds.
