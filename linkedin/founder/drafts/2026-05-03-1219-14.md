## Post

73% of the B2B buying journey happens before a buyer ever contacts a vendor.

That number changes how I think about almost everything in marketing.

---

By the time someone fills out a form, requests a demo, or replies to an outbound email — they've already done most of the work.

They visited review sites.

They asked peers in Slack channels what tools they use.

They read comparison articles.

They asked AI tools to build them a shortlist.

They watched a founder's content and formed an opinion.

Their mental shortlist is basically formed.

The demo is often just a verification step for a decision they've already half-made.

---

This is what researchers call the dark funnel — the portion of the buying journey that happens in places your marketing stack can't track.

The problem: most marketing teams are measured on what happens at the end of that journey.

MQLs. Form fills. Demo requests.

Those are real signals. But they represent the last 27% of a decision process that mostly happened somewhere else.

---

The founders I see winning on organic growth have shifted their thinking.

They stopped asking "how do I capture more leads?" and started asking "how do I show up during the research phase?"

That means publishing content without a gate — because ungated content travels further and gets consumed by buyers who would never fill out a form.

That means showing up where peer conversations happen — communities, forums, events, the places AI tools crawl when building answers.

That means building brand familiarity before someone is ready to buy — so when their shortlist forms, you're already on it.

---

The mental model shift: marketing isn't primarily about converting interest into leads.

It's about building recognition with people who aren't ready to be leads yet.

Most of them will never become leads.

But the ones who do — they already know you. The trust is there. The close rate is higher. The onboarding is faster.

The dark funnel isn't a problem to solve.

It's a phase to earn.
