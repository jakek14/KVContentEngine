## Post

The brand vs. performance debate is framed wrong.

Seven in ten marketers say they're likely to increase performance spend and reduce brand investment this year.

I get it. Performance is measurable. CFOs like measurable. When budgets get tight, you fund the channel you can justify with a spreadsheet.

But here's what the data actually shows:

When your brand is more easily recalled, your performance campaigns work harder for less money.

That's not a soft marketing take. It's a mechanical relationship.

---

Performance marketing doesn't operate in isolation. Every ad you run is filtered through a lens the viewer already has: "have I heard of these people?"

When the answer is no — your click rates are lower, your conversion rates are lower, and your CAC is higher.

Brand isn't competing with performance. It lowers the cost of everything else you do.

---

The founders I've seen get this right treat brand spend as an investment in making their performance spend more efficient — not as a luxury for when they have budget left over.

Here's the mental model I use:

Performance marketing is renting attention.
Brand marketing is building a reason for people to care when they see you.

One delivers results this quarter. The other determines how much those results cost next quarter.

---

The question isn't "brand or performance?"

It's: at what stage is your audience?

Early stage — brand creates recognition. Performance converts people who already know who you are.

Skip the brand work and your performance campaigns are fishing in a pond where nobody's heard of you. The CPMs are the same. The conversion rates aren't.

Both matter. The split should follow your stage — not your CFO's comfort level with spreadsheet attribution.

If you've been cutting brand to fund performance and CAC is still rising, that might be why.
