## Post

70% of businesses will prefer usage-based pricing over per-seat by end of 2026.

That's Gartner's call. And honestly, I'm not surprised.

---

Per-seat pricing was always a proxy for value — not value itself.

You're not paying for how useful the tool is. You're paying for how many people have access to it. Your costs scale with headcount, not outcomes.

Usage-based breaks that.

With usage-based pricing, you pay for what you actually use. Which means the vendor now has skin in the game. If the product isn't delivering, you use it less. Your bill drops. You don't need a negotiation — the model self-corrects.

---

Here's what changes strategically when you make the switch:

**Acquisition gets harder.** No more "just add 10 seats and see if it sticks." Customers need to actually activate to generate revenue.

**Churn has nowhere to hide.** Low engagement isn't a lagging indicator anymore — it shows up immediately in revenue.

**Retention becomes your growth lever.** The accounts that expand are the ones genuinely getting value. They self-select.

---

The companies winning on usage-based right now share one thing: they built for activation, not just sign-up.

Getting someone in the door isn't the play anymore.

Getting them to the "this actually works" moment — fast — is the whole game.

Most SaaS products still aren't built for that. Most founders are still optimizing the funnel above the fold instead of the product experience below it.

The pricing model will force the conversation. One way or another.
