## Post

CMO turnover is the highest it's been in years.

That's not random.

Most companies treat marketing as a headcount decision — hire the right person, run a playbook, hit targets. When targets miss, swap the executive.

The problem: that model treats marketing as an execution function. And execution functions are replaceable.

---

The best-run companies I've watched treat marketing completely differently.

Not as a department to manage. As a product function to build.

The questions aren't "what campaigns are we running?" They're "what do our buyers understand about their problem before they know we exist?" and "what message architecture drives pipeline that doesn't depend on any one channel?"

Those questions don't reset when a CMO leaves. They live in the operating system.

---

There's a version of marketing that's durable and a version that isn't.

The durable version: a clear ICP, a message that resonates, a content strategy that builds real authority over time, channels you own instead of rent. This doesn't change with leadership. It compounds.

The non-durable version: campaigns. Playbooks borrowed from what worked somewhere else. Activity metrics. Leads that don't close because no one built trust before the call.

The non-durable version is what most companies replace their CMO over. And then rebuild. And replace again.

---

The founders I respect most in 2026 think about marketing the way they think about product-market fit — not as a box to check, but as a continuous, compounding discovery process.

What do buyers actually believe? What language do they use when they describe the problem? Where does our point of view create a reaction?

That discovery never ends. You iterate it forever, the same way you iterate the product.

The founders who treat it that way don't have a "marketing problem." They have an unfair advantage.
