## Post

Dozens of companies have crossed $10M ARR with teams of five or fewer.

This isn't a curiosity. It's a structural shift in what's actually possible — and most founders are still running the old playbook.

---

The assumption underneath most startup thinking is that headcount equals capacity.

You need a sales team to hit $1M ARR. A marketing team to drive awareness. Engineers to ship features. Customer success to reduce churn. Operations to keep it together.

That assumption held when humans were doing all of the work.

AI has compressed every one of those departments.

---

The companies hitting $10M with five people aren't doing it by working brutal hours across every function.

They're doing it by being deliberate about what a small team with the right tools can actually do — and staying focused there.

The interesting part isn't the technology. It's what staying lean allows:

You keep equity. Every round you don't raise is dilution you don't take. Ownership stays where it belongs.

You make decisions faster. A five-person team doesn't need alignment meetings. The person with the most context makes the call.

You stay close to customers. There's no account management layer between you and the real signal.

You stay profitable. Revenue goes to the people who built the thing — not headcount that needs to justify itself every quarter.

---

The tradeoffs are real, and I'm not pretending otherwise.

Certain sales cycles require relationship coverage at scale. Enterprise deals need headcount behind them. Brand credibility in some markets assumes visible team size. Some markets you simply cannot win with five people.

The question isn't "should I always stay lean?"

The question is whether scaling headcount is actually the bottleneck at your current stage — or whether you're adding people because that's what the playbook says you're supposed to do.

---

For a long time, that choice was forced.

You couldn't compete at certain scales without headcount. You hired or you fell behind.

AI changed that forcing function.

Now staying small is genuinely a choice. And the moment it becomes a choice, it's worth making deliberately — not by default.

What would it actually cost you to stay at five people for another 12 months?

And what would you keep that you'd otherwise give away?
