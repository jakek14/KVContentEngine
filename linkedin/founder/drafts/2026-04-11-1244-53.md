## Post

A single ratio tells you more about your company's health than almost anything else.

LTV:CAC. And the standard just moved.

---

For years, 3:1 was the benchmark. $3 of lifetime value for every $1 spent acquiring a customer. Hit that, and investors viewed your unit economics as fundamentally sound.

In 2026, the expectation is closer to 4:1.

CAC has jumped 40-60% since 2023. Investors are more demanding on payback periods. The definition of "efficient" has shifted upward.

---

Here's what I find interesting about this shift:

A 4:1 LTV:CAC ratio isn't just a fundraising milestone. It forces you to think differently about how you build the entire company from the start.

You can improve the ratio two ways:

Lower CAC — better targeting, higher conversion, more organic demand.

Raise LTV — better retention, expansion revenue, upsells, referrals.

Most teams only pull on the CAC side. They optimize ads. Tighten funnels. Negotiate CPCs. It feels like progress because spend is trackable.

But the highest-leverage move is almost always on the LTV side.

---

Companies running 5:1 or 6:1 LTV:CAC ratios aren't just better at acquisition. They built something customers don't leave easily.

Onboarding that actually changes behavior.

Expansion paths that create compounding value over time.

Success motions that give customers a real reason to stay — and a reason to tell others.

---

A business where customers stay and grow is a fundamentally different kind of business than one where customers churn and need to be replaced.

The first kind has leverage. The second is a treadmill.

---

The ratio is a compass. It tells you which one you're building.

What's your current LTV:CAC — and is it trending the right direction?
