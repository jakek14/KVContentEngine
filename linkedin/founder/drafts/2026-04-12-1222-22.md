## Post

Cursor just crossed $2B in annualized revenue.

$0 to $29.3 billion in 43 months.

The fastest value creation in SaaS history. By a lot.

---

I think about this a lot — not because I'm building a dev tool, but because it's the clearest data point we have about what AI actually does to software business model assumptions.

The old rule: building software required expensive engineers and long timelines. Speed was a function of how many people you could hire.

Cursor broke that. Not because they hired better engineers — because they built a tool that made engineers faster. The value they capture is the productivity delta.

---

Here's what I find more interesting for founders who aren't building AI tools:

Every company using Cursor now ships faster. Every non-technical founder using vibe coding tools ships faster.

The execution gap between funded and unfunded teams just got measurably smaller.

But the strategic gap — knowing what to build and for whom — didn't change at all.

---

AI tools give you speed.

They don't give you insight into your customer. They don't tell you which problem is worth solving. They don't know why a cohort churns or why a segment stays.

The founders extracting the most from AI-assisted development in 2026 are the ones who front-loaded the hard thinking — the product clarity, the customer understanding, the hypothesis about why this matters.

Know what you're building, who it's for, and why they'll pay for it.

Then hand it off to the tools.

The tools execute. The clarity has to come from you.

---

Cursor's $29B tells me AI is a real accelerant.

It doesn't tell me what to build.

That's still the hard part.
