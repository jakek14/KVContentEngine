## Post

When you build without a marketing budget, something useful happens.

You can't pay your way to distribution.

Which means you have to earn it.

---

I didn't have a playbook for this when I started. Most founders don't.

The default move is to try everything at once — SEO, paid ads, cold outreach, partnerships, content — and see what sticks. Which mostly means draining runway without getting clean signal on what actually moved the needle.

The constraint that ended up teaching me the most was running out of budget before running out of ideas.

When you genuinely can't keep spending, you start looking for what grew without it. What channels kept sending people back without dollars behind them. Who shared the product without being asked. What content ranked on its own. What subject lines got replies from strangers.

Those signals are the real thing. They're repeatable growth.

---

There's a useful distinction between rented distribution and owned distribution.

Every channel that requires you to keep paying to keep growing is rented. The second you stop funding it, it stops.

Email lists. Organic search authority. Word of mouth. Communities where people know your name. Customers who come back without a retargeting ad.

These channels aren't free to build — they take time and real attention. But they compound differently. They don't turn off.

The founders who build owned distribution before they have the budget to shortcut it end up in a structurally different position. Not just because they have channels that compound — but because they understand what their audience actually cares about at a level that can't be bought.

---

Here's what I mean by that.

When you earn distribution the hard way — before you can amplify anything — you learn what resonates. Not what looks good in a spreadsheet. Not what a benchmark report says should work. What the specific people you're trying to reach actually respond to.

That knowledge doesn't transfer from a competitor's playbook. It doesn't come from a course on growth marketing. It comes from building the thing, talking to users, watching what clicks, and iterating until you find the version that earns attention without forcing it.

Every founder gets this education eventually.

The ones who built lean got it earlier. Earlier compounds.

---

When I look at the founders I respect most — the ones running efficient, growing companies that aren't dependent on an ad platform not changing its pricing model next quarter — they all did some version of this.

They found channels that worked before they had money to scale them.

Then they scaled them.

That sequencing matters more than most people give it credit for.

---

The channels you discover organically are the ones worth scaling.

Everything else is buying distribution you never truly owned.
