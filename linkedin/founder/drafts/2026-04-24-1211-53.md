## Post

Gartner predicts 70% of businesses will prefer usage-based pricing over per-seat by end of 2026.

That number would have seemed extreme three years ago.

It doesn't anymore.

---

The per-seat model was built on a specific assumption: that the number of users was a reasonable proxy for the value your tool creates.

That was never perfectly true. But it was close enough — when humans did all the work, more users roughly meant more output.

AI broke that assumption.

Today, one person with the right tools can do work that used to require five. One agent can handle tasks that required a team.

The connection between seat count and value created has dissolved.

Charging per user in that environment is charging for the wrong thing.

---

Usage-based pricing aligns incentives differently.

You charge for what the customer actually consumes: API calls, contacts processed, reports generated, records identified.

When your customers succeed — when they use the product and get real value — your revenue goes up. When they don't, they don't pay as much.

The pressure to drive adoption stays where it belongs: on you.

---

The counterintuitive part: usage-based models often grow revenue faster.

Instead of fighting over the right seat count at renewal, you grow alongside your customer's actual usage.

Their growth becomes your growth.

---

What this requires from founders:

A clear value metric — the one unit of output your customer actually cares about. Not "users." Not "seats." The real thing your product delivers.

Pricing that scales fairly — a baseline that doesn't punish small teams for getting started, and doesn't cap upside when they scale.

The systems to measure it — usage-based sounds simple, but metering accurately is a real operational investment.

---

The shift is already happening.

43% of SaaS companies are using hybrid models today. That number is expected to hit 61% by end of 2026.

If you're building a product now and defaulting to per-seat because "that's how SaaS pricing works" — it's worth questioning that assumption before you ship.

The pricing model you choose is a strategy.

Make sure it's built around value delivered, not how many people are logged in.
