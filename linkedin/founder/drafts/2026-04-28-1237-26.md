## Post

The metric that predicts your next funding round isn't ARR.

It's NRR — net revenue retention.

Companies above 120% NRR grow 83% faster than the median.

They command 2–2.5x higher valuation multiples at Series B and C.

Expansion revenue — upsells, upgrades, usage growth from existing customers — now accounts for 40–50% of new ARR at the fastest-growing SaaS companies.

Yet most founders spend 80% of their growth budget chasing new customers.

Here's what the math actually tells you:

NRR of 90%: you're losing revenue before acquisition even opens the door. Every new customer partially offsets what you're bleeding from existing ones. Treadmill.

NRR of 110%: your existing base grows on its own. New customers accelerate a machine that's already compounding.

NRR of 120%+: you have a fundamentally different company. Investors know it. The valuation conversation happens in an entirely different comp set.

The founder obsession with CAC makes sense — acquisition is visible, trackable, controllable. You can see the pipeline. You can manage the funnel.

NRR is the invisible counterforce that determines whether all that acquisition spend actually builds something durable.

Three things that move NRR up:

1. Build for expansion, not just activation. Are there natural reasons customers pay more as they extract more value? Usage levers, seat growth, feature upgrades.

2. Make onboarding create habits, not just accounts. The first 90 days set the trajectory. Most companies treat onboarding like the last step of the sale rather than the first step of the relationship.

3. Understand why customers stay and why they leave — from behavioral signals and real conversations, not exit surveys sent after they've already decided.

The companies I've watched compound fastest share one trait: their growth team and their CS team are talking about the same customer.

Acquisition and retention aren't separate strategies.

They're one strategy with a gap in the middle that most companies quietly ignore.
