## Post

The definition of "lead" is holding B2B companies back.

Most sales teams define a lead as someone who filled out a form.

Here's what that definition misses:

---

The person who visited your pricing page four times this month.

The account that read three of your case studies in two days.

The company whose employees keep appearing in your blog analytics week after week.

None of them filled out a form.

All of them showed intent.

---

The reason most B2B teams are struggling with pipeline quality isn't that there aren't enough interested buyers.

It's that their definition of "interested" is too narrow.

Form fills are a proxy for intent — and a lagging one. By the time someone fills out a form, they've already done most of their research. You're one of several options they're evaluating simultaneously.

The teams building pipeline early are capturing intent before the form fill. They're identifying which accounts are actively in-market, reaching out with relevant context, and starting conversations when the buyer is still forming their consideration set — not when they're ready to compare finalists.

---

The 2026 B2B buyer leaves intent signals everywhere.

On your website. In your content analytics. In email engagement data.

The companies treating these signals as first-class leads — not just "anonymous traffic" — are building a different category of pipeline.

Higher close rates.

Shorter sales cycles.

More relationships, fewer transactions.

---

The form isn't where the lead begins.

The interest is where the lead begins.
