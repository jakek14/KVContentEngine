## Title

Seats Don't Scale Anymore

## Subtitle

The pricing model that built the SaaS industry is getting replaced — and the window to get ahead of it is closing faster than most founders realize.

## Post

Most SaaS founders didn't choose per-seat pricing. It chose them.

When SaaS emerged in the early 2000s, the logic was obvious. Software was replacing people who did things manually — every person on your team needed a login to do their job. Charging per user matched how the customer thought about the cost. It scaled predictably. It was easy to sell, easy to explain, and easy to model in a spreadsheet.

For two decades, it worked. Most of the companies that built the SaaS industry — Salesforce, Hubspot, Zendesk, the whole stack — were built on per-seat pricing.

Then something changed.

---

## The Premise Just Broke

The per-seat model rests on a core assumption: the more people at a company use your software, the more valuable it is to them.

That was true when software served individual humans doing work. One salesperson, one seat. Ten salespeople, ten seats. Revenue growth and usage growth were the same curve.

AI broke that assumption.

When an AI agent can handle the tasks that used to require a named employee with a login — drafting outreach, triaging support tickets, analyzing campaign data, generating reports — the connection between headcount and software value snaps.

A company with ten AI agents and two human employees might be extracting more value from your product than a company with fifty seats. If you're charging per seat, you're leaving most of that value on the table — or worse, you're pricing yourself out of the sale because the buyer can't justify ten seats when they're running a lean AI-powered team.

This isn't hypothetical. This is what's showing up in the data.

---

## What the Numbers Say

Per-seat pricing dropped from 21% to 15% of SaaS companies in just twelve months.

Gartner predicts that 70% of businesses will prefer usage-based over per-seat models by 2026. OpenView's research confirms the direction: 61% of SaaS companies now use usage-based pricing, up from 45% in 2021. Hybrid models — a base fee plus variable usage or outcome components — are at 43% and projected to hit 61% by end of year.

The model isn't just declining. It's collapsing.

And there's another data point that should matter to every founder thinking about retention: companies with outcome-based pricing components see 31% higher customer retention and 21% higher satisfaction scores than those on pure per-seat models.

That last number is the one I find most interesting. When your pricing aligns with the value a customer actually gets, they stick around longer and they're happier. Of course they are. They're paying for results, not access.

---

## The Three Models Replacing It

If per-seat is fading, what's taking over?

**Usage-based pricing** is the most straightforward replacement. You price on the unit of work the software performs — emails sent, API calls made, contacts identified, workflows executed. The customer's bill scales with how much they use the product. When value goes up, revenue goes up. When usage slows, the pricing adjusts. It's aligned.

The risk with pure usage-based is unpredictability for both sides. Customers hate billing surprises. Vendors hate revenue variance. This is why most companies are landing on hybrid models rather than pure usage-based.

**Outcome-based pricing** is the boldest move — and the hardest to execute. Intercom charges $0.99 per resolved support ticket. You're not paying for the platform. You're paying for the result. This model forces vendors to actually care about outcomes, not just adoption metrics. And when it works, it commands premium pricing because the ROI is undeniable.

The challenge: you need to be able to measure outcomes clearly and consistently, and you need enough trust with the customer that they believe the measurement is fair.

**Hybrid models** are where most of the market is landing. A base subscription fee for predictability, combined with usage or outcome components that align billing with value. The base fee gives the customer a budget anchor. The variable component gives you expansion revenue as usage grows. It's the model that solves for both sides.

43% of SaaS companies are already here. That number will likely hit 60% by the end of the year.

---

## If You're Building Today

If you're a founder just starting out — or even in the early stages of product-market fit — you have a rare opportunity: you get to choose your pricing model before it gets baked into your customer relationships and your internal financial model.

Here's what I'd focus on:

**Find your value metric first.** Before you think about pricing tiers or seat counts, ask: what is the one unit of output or outcome that correlates most directly with the value our customers get? Is it contacts identified? Emails delivered? Reports generated? Leads qualified? Whatever it is, that's what you should price on.

The companies that get this right tend to see faster expansion revenue, higher retention, and an easier sales conversation — because the pricing makes obvious sense to the buyer.

**Don't default to per-seat because it's familiar.** The familiarity of per-seat pricing is also why it's a trap. It's easy to model, easy to pitch, and completely disconnected from value for most AI-assisted workflows in 2026. You're starting from scratch — don't anchor to a model that's eroding.

**Design for expansion.** The best pricing models don't just monetize the initial sale — they grow automatically as the customer extracts more value. Usage-based and hybrid models do this by nature. Per-seat models do it only when you hire more people, which is increasingly not happening.

---

## If You're Repricing Tomorrow

This is harder, and I want to be honest about that.

If you have existing customers on per-seat contracts, moving them to a usage-based or outcome-based model is a genuine operational challenge. Some will see their bills go up. Some will feel like the rules changed on them. Transitioning pricing mid-relationship requires trust, clear communication, and usually a lengthy migration window.

But the companies avoiding this conversation aren't avoiding the problem. They're just delaying it.

The customers who aren't using your product enough to justify seat-based pricing are already at churn risk. And the customers who are using it heavily — who would pay more under usage-based pricing — are actually subsidizing the ones who aren't. That math gets worse over time.

The transition is painful. The alternative is being locked into a pricing model that's slowly working against you.

---

## The Bigger Principle

Pricing is not a sales conversation. It's a product decision.

The pricing model you choose tells customers what you think you're selling. Per-seat says: we're selling access. Usage-based says: we're selling capacity. Outcome-based says: we're selling results.

In 2026, the companies winning the pricing conversation are the ones who figured out what they're actually selling — and built a model that makes that obvious.

Seats were a proxy for value when the relationship between headcount and software usage was direct. That relationship is gone.

The next generation of SaaS pricing will be built on what the software actually does for the business — not on how many people happen to log in.

---

The founders who build their pricing models around value metrics from day one are going to have a significant structural advantage over the ones who have to reprice later.

This is one of those moments where the window to get ahead of a structural shift is still open.

It won't be for long.
