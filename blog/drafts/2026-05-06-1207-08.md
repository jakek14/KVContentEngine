## Title

The SaaS Meltdown Is Real. Here's Who Makes It Through.

## Subtitle

Growth-at-all-costs is officially dead. The companies that survive aren't the ones who pivoted to AI — they're the ones who built the unsexy fundamentals.

## Post

There's a version of 2026 that looks like a SaaS catastrophe, depending on who you're watching.

Valuations compressed. Acquirers pulling back. Companies that raised at 30x ARR multiples in 2021 now running out of runway on paper that no longer makes sense. Investors who used to compete to fund "land and expand" plays are now asking different questions — harder questions — about unit economics they probably should have been asking all along.

Chamath called it the "Great SaaS Meltdown." Alex Lieberman put it cleaner: "Bad SaaS is dead. And it always has been."

Both of them are right. But I think there's a more useful frame — one that tells you not just what's dying, but what survives.

---

**The Bet That Stopped Working**

The last decade of B2B SaaS ran on a specific bet: that if you grew fast enough, everything else would work itself out.

You'd acquire customers aggressively, even at a loss, because the lifetime value would eventually justify the CAC. You'd build features faster than competitors and figure out retention later. You'd raise enough capital to outrun the unit economics for long enough that the market would reward the growth rate.

For a specific window — roughly 2017 to 2022 — that bet paid off for a lot of companies.

Then the window closed.

The public market data tells the story clearly now. Median SaaS growth today is 12.7%. Median EBITDA margins are 22.6%. The market isn't pricing growth anymore. It's pricing discipline — proven retention, expanding revenue from existing customers, cost structures that don't require permanent external subsidy.

The companies struggling aren't struggling because the market changed. They're struggling because the bet they made stopped working. And they made the bet because for a long time, it worked.

---

**The Three Failure Modes**

When I look at what's actually going wrong for struggling SaaS companies in 2026, it tends to cluster around three problems.

The first is feature inflation. Somewhere between 2020 and 2024, the competitive response to every threat became "build more features." Roadmaps stretched. Products got complicated. The promise was: we'll be the platform, the all-in-one, the last tool you need to buy. What actually happened: customers got confused, onboarding got harder, churn crept up as the core use case got buried under capabilities they didn't need.

The second is CAC creep nobody modeled. Customer acquisition costs have climbed steadily across paid channels — Google, LinkedIn, Meta — while conversion rates on the same channels stalled or declined. The companies that built growth plans assuming 2020 CAC would hold into 2026 are now looking at LTV/CAC ratios that don't work. And the playbook of "just spend more" doesn't fix a math problem.

The third is retention treated as an afterthought. Most SaaS companies built their acquisition engines first and bolted on retention programs later. The assumption was that if the product was good enough, customers would stay. Some did. But "good enough" isn't the bar anymore. Customers have options. Switching costs are lower. If the product doesn't demonstrate ongoing value — clearly, repeatedly — they leave.

Bain's data makes the cost of that mistake concrete: a 5% improvement in customer retention increases profits somewhere between 25% and 95%. Most companies left that lever untouched for years.

---

**Who Isn't Sweating This**

Here's what I find interesting: not everyone is under pressure.

There's a category of SaaS company that's watching this moment with something closer to calm than panic. They're not the ones who raised the most money or hit the fastest growth curves. They're the ones who never needed growth to justify the cost structure.

Bootstrapped founders who solved a specific problem, built close to the customer, and kept spending below revenue. Companies that hit $1M ARR by delivering something people couldn't afford to cancel. Teams that treated retention as the product, not the support function.

ChartMogul analyzed 2,500+ SaaS companies and found something worth sitting with: the top quartile of bootstrapped companies reach $1M ARR only four months behind their VC-backed peers. Four months. That's the speed premium early equity was supposed to buy.

For a winner-take-all market, four months matters. For most B2B SaaS, it doesn't.

The founders who chose to bootstrap — or who raised sparingly and late — didn't just survive the meltdown. They were never exposed to it. Because the meltdown is specifically a story about companies that built on borrowed time and borrowed assumptions.

---

**The AI Wildcard**

There's a version of 2026 where AI changes this entirely — where the combination of AI-assisted development, lean teams, and capital efficiency creates a new category of sustainable, high-performing SaaS companies that look nothing like the 2019 playbook.

I think that version is real. But I'd be careful about conflating "using AI" with "being protected from bad fundamentals."

AI tools have collapsed the cost of building. What used to take a team of ten can be done with a team of three. The barrier to shipping a product is lower than it's ever been. That's genuinely good news for founders who want to move fast without raising enormous rounds.

What AI doesn't change: whether customers stay, whether the unit economics work, whether you built something people actually need. The meltdown isn't a story about technology. It's a story about business models.

The companies winning with AI are using it to do more with less — not to grow faster with worse metrics.

---

**What Actually Survives**

The simplest version of the answer: companies where the value delivered is clear enough that customers don't need to be convinced to renew.

That sounds obvious. It isn't. Most SaaS companies, if they're honest, have a retention problem that's really a value communication problem. The product does something valuable. The customer doesn't always connect the dots between "this tool" and "that outcome." Renewals become negotiation cycles. Churn becomes the gap between what was promised and what was felt.

The companies making it through built a shorter path between the product and the outcome. Onboarding that gets users to value in days, not months. Success metrics that map directly to what the customer cares about. Expansion revenue that happens naturally because the product keeps proving its worth.

The fundamentals aren't complicated. They were just optional for a long time because growth covered for them.

They're not optional anymore.

---

The meltdown isn't the end of SaaS. It's the end of a specific bet that was always eventually going to stop working.

The companies coming out the other side built something simpler: products that work for the people buying them, data that reflects reality, and customers who stay because the product earns it.

That was always the game. 2026 just stopped letting you pretend otherwise.
